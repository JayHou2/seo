London student rent zones: where should students live on a budget?

London rent decisions are really commute decisions. A cheaper room can become expensive if it creates long transfers, late-night stress, or a contract that does not match the academic year.

Start with zones, then check the actual route

Zones 1 and 2 are convenient but often expensive. Zones 2 and 3 are common starting points for students who want a better balance. Farther zones can work if the direct route is reliable and the rent savings are meaningful.

What to compare before signing

  • Monthly rent and whether bills are included.
  • Door-to-door commute to campus, not just distance on a map.
  • Night and weekend transport options.
  • Deposit, guarantor requirements, and contract length.
  • Furniture, laundry, heating, and internet costs.

Halls vs private rent

University halls can be simpler for the first year because setup, bills, and social access are clearer. Private rent can be cheaper or more flexible, but only if you understand the total monthly cost and move-in cash requirement.

Should students live as close to campus as possible?

Not always. The best option is the cheapest place that still gives you a reliable, safe, and tolerable commute.
